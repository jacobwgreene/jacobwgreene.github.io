Tracing Counter-Rhetorics of Augmented Reality

"The elements of rhetorical situation simply bleed."
-Jenny Rice, "Unframing Models of Public Distribution: From Rhetorical Situation to Rhetorical Ecologies"

For part of my research into augmented reality (AR) as an emerging writing technology, I am tracing different activist/artistic projects that configure AR as a counter-rhetorical genre. My theoretical approach for this research emerges partially from Clay Spinuzzi's claim that new genres emerge out of "destabilizations" between existing genres and the actor's rhetorical purpose. For Spinuzzi, such destabilizations force writers “not only to reinterpret existing genres, but also to adapt new genres” (Tracing Genres, 67). Spinuzzi writes that a genre should be studied not at a specific period in time but rather “as it develops over a period of months or years.” In order to trace the diachronic development of AR as a counter-rhteorical genre, I draw from Laurie Gries' methodology of "iconographic tracking." Gries' method advocates for a productive dialectic between “data hoarding” (collecting data through web and image searches) and “data mining” (analyzing this data for patterns and trends) so that a researcher’s understanding of a particular cultural phenomenon is constantly informed by the movement of the phenomenon itself and not just the initial concerns of the researcher.
