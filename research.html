Research

In 2015, over fifty-two percent of internet traffic took place within mobile devices, officially eclipsing the prior dominance of the traditional desktop interface. What are the rhetorical and compositional implications of this shift to mobile, ubqiquitous, and wearable computing?

In general, this question undergirds the majority of my research into emerging writing technologies such as augmented/mixed reality. Coming from the discipline of digtial rhetoric and composition studies, I am specifically interested in how these technologies are being configured rhetorically by both designers and users. To this end, I frequently write and teach about how mobile writing technologies can be leveraged to alter and/or subvert perceptions of a physical location, object, text, or image. In addition to my writing and teaching, my research into mobile augmented reality also takes a critical making approach, and in my time as the Augmented Reality Criticisms Coordinator for Trace Innovation, I have developed several mobile augmented reality applications. To learn more about my work in mobile AR with Trace, visit the Trace ARCs website.
